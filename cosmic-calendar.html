The clever bit

13 × 28 only reaches 364 days, but a year is ~365¼. So the leftover 1¼ days become festival days that sit outside the normal week — a "Year's Day" belonging to no weekday. That's the trick that locks every date to the same weekday forever without ever breaking the 7-day week (the snag that sank earlier reforms).

The honest bit

The Moon has never divided evenly into a year — for anyone, ever. Instead of faking it, this calendar simply shows you the real Moon beside the dates and admits the two don't line up. The Sun runs the calendar; the Moon keeps its own counsel alongside.

What you'd actually feel: you'd always know, just from the date, where you are in the year's wheel — "it's Highsun, near the longest day." Your schedule would be predictable, and the calendar would never drift from the real seasons.

The fair trade-off: 13 doesn't divide into 4, so there are no clean business "quarters" — and the whole world would have to agree to switch. That's exactly why Gregorian, flaws and all, is so hard to dethrone.

The Grievance

You are right. The calendar on your wall is a committee's compromise, not a map of the sky.

The Gregorian calendar keeps excellent arithmetic — but it has quietly divorced itself from the heavens. Its months are the fossilised politics of Rome: unequal, irregular, named for emperors and for numbers that no longer match (Sep‑, Oct‑, Nov‑, Dec‑ — the 7th through 10th months, marooned at positions 9 through 12). Its year begins on no astronomical event at all — eleven cold, arbitrary days after the solstice. Its weekday wanders, so every year the grid must be reprinted. It is precise about counting and indifferent to meaning.

FLAW 01

An arbitrary New Year

1 January marks nothing in the sky — not a solstice, equinox, perihelion, or rising star. The year begins in a void.

FLAW 02

Irregular months

28, 30, 31 days in no pattern; lengths set by Caesar and Augustus' egos, not by Moon or Sun. No month is a lunation.

FLAW 03

A wandering weekday

The same date lands on a different weekday each year. The grid never repeats; the calendar is disposable by design.

FLAW 04

Seasons that slip

Even the clever 400‑year leap rule leaves a residual error of ~27 seconds a year — one whole day adrift every ~3,030 years.

FLAW 05

Numbers that lie

December means "tenth month." October means "eighth." The names are the scar tissue of a calendar reformed and never healed.

FLAW 06

The sky forgotten

Nothing on the page tells you where Earth is in its orbit, which stars the Sun hides among, or what the Moon is doing tonight.

Thirteen Equal Moons

The Months of the Year

Each month is identical — exactly 28 days, exactly four weeks (Kindling · Waxing · Waning · Gathering) — so every date keeps the same weekday forever. But where Cotsworth left his thirteenth month blandly named "Sol," we have re‑themed all thirteen to the turning of the season, the nature of the light, and the constellation the Sun truly occupies — corrected for precession, and including Ophiuchus, the thirteenth sign, a fitting guest in a thirteen‑month year.

The Wheel of the Year

Eight Seasonal Stations

These are not invented holidays — they are the eight astronomically real hinge‑points of Earth's orbit: the two solstices (Sun highest and lowest), the two equinoxes (day equal to night), and the four cross‑quarter days exactly halfway between them. Honoured by Stonehenge and Newgrange, by the Celts and the Inca, they are the bones the calendar hangs upon, so the date can never drift from the season.

An Honest Reckoning of the Moon

The Lunar Track

Here we refuse to lie. Thirteen months of 28 days are not lunar months — the true Moon, new to new, takes 29.53059 days, and a year holds 12.36827 of them: an irrational, unrepeatable number. Every lunisolar calendar in history — Babylonian, Hebrew, Chinese, Hindu — wrestled with this and could only paper over it with leap months.

So the Solstice Wheel does not force the Moon into the months. Instead it carries a parallel Lunar Track that simply reports the truth: the phase, the Moon's age in days, the illuminated fraction, the lunation count since the year began, and the Metonic Golden Number — your year's slot in the 19‑year cycle in which 235 lunations almost exactly refill 19 solar years, the same cycle the Antikythera mechanism computed two thousand years ago. Eclipse seasons are flagged from the 18‑year‑11‑day Saros.

The Sun owns the calendar. The Moon keeps its own honest counsel beside it. Neither corrupts the other.

Driftless by Design

Read From the Sky, Not From a Formula

The year begins on the observed December solstice at the reference meridian — the moment itself, measured, not approximated. Because the start is re‑anchored to a real sky‑event every single year, the seasons can never wander. No 4/100/400 kludge is needed at all. The sky keeps the time. A leap year is simply any year in which 366 dawns fall between two solstices.

How the remainder is kept. The 13 months hold 364 days — a whole 52 weeks. The leftover ~1.24 days live outside the week, as festival days that belong to no month and no weekday:

· Solstice Day ("Firstlight Eve") — the solstice instant itself, the seam between the old year's last day and the new year's first.
· Midsummer Day ("the Long Light") — added only in leap years, at the June solstice, so the extra day falls at the bright pole, symmetrically opposite the dark.

Because these days sit off the seven‑day cycle, the unbroken week — and the Sabbath — is never interrupted. That single fix cures the religious objection that killed the Cotsworth and Achelis reforms at the League of Nations and the UN.
